Classification of Tissue

Composition of Tissue

  • Cells
    • Cell organelles (viz., mitochondria and secretomes/exosomes) function as regulators, and can be used as therapeutic agents
  • Extracellular matrix
    • ECM can regulate cell processes via integrins
  • Regulators
    • Chemical
      • Diffusible: autocrine, paracrine, and endocrine factors (viz., growth factors)
      • Non-Diffusible: ECM ligands for integrins
    • Physical: mechanical strain (including extracorporeal pressure waves)

Unit Cell Processes

  • Mitosis
  • Migration
  • Synthesis
    • Extracellular matrix molecules
    • Enzymes (e.g., matrix metalloproteinases/collagenases)
    • Regulatory molecules (e.g., growth factors)
    • Nucleotides
  • Contraction
  • Endocytosis
  • Exocytosis
  • Apoptosis
  • Differentiation

Tissue Types

  • Connective Tissue *
  • Epithelia *
  • Muscle *
  • Nerve *
  • Extracellular matrix continuous; cells bind to ECM molecules via integrins
  • Some tissues also cell continuous
  • Cell continuous
  • Muscle and nerve cells are surrounded by a basal lamina*
  • Epithelial cells connected to each other by cell adhesion molecules (CAMs); basal epithelial cells are attached to basement membrane

Extracellular Matrices (ECMs)

  • Extracellular Matrix (3-D, interstitial, matrix)
    • ECM molecules fill the space among separated cells in connective tissues
    • Molecules include:
      • Collagens (I, II, III, IX, XI – among 29 types)
      • Glycosaminoglycans (GAGs)
      • Adhesion proteins (e.g., fibronectin)
      • Other ECM components
  • Basement Membrane / Basal Lamina (2-D matrix)
    • Defined ECM membrane (50–300 nm thick)
    • Separates certain tissues (BM) and surrounds certain cells (BL)
    • Functions:
      • Separates epithelial and connective tissue
        (e.g., epidermis from dermis in skin)
      • Separates different epithelial tissues
        (e.g., urothelium from endothelium in kidney glomerulus)
    • BL surrounds muscle and nerve cells, and some connective tissue cells
    • Molecules include:
      • Collagens (IV, XVIII)
      • Laminin
      • Heparan sulfate
      • Others
  • Pericellular Matrix
    • Composed of BM molecules
    • Occurs around certain connective tissue cells (e.g., chondrocytes)

Parts of an Organ: Parenchyma and Stroma

  • Parenchyma is the functional part of an organ.
    • The parenchymal cell provides the essential function of the organ.
  • Stroma is the structural tissue of organs, often referred to as connective tissue.
    • The stromal cell supports the function of the parenchymal cell.
    • Often a connective tissue cell.
      • Provides support through a mechanical function, by providing paracrine factors, or by offering other roles (including phagocytic activity)
      • Fibroblasts and pericytes are among the most common types of stromal cells.

Features of a Tissue/Organ that Affect Its Ability to Regenerate

  • Vascularity
    • Positive
      • Provides a fibrin clot as a provisional matrix/scaffold that enables cell migration into the defect
      • Provides platelets that release regulatory molecules (growth factors)
      • Provides stem cells: pericytes and endothelial cells
    • Negative
      • Endothelial cells differentiate into myofibroblasts that synthesize collagen and contract the ECM, thus forming scar
  • Presence of stem and progenitor cells
    • Includes cells other than endothelial cells and pericytes
  • Density of the ECM of connective tissues
    • Can inhibit migration of host cells to the site
  • Presence of molecular regulators
    • Can be stimulators or inhibitors of repair and regeneration
